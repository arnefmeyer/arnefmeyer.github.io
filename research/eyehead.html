Head and eye movements in freely moving mice

The mouse has become one of the most prominent species in vision research, in part because enormous progress has been made in the last few years in developing new tools to monitor and manipulate neural activity in mice. However, how the freely-moving mouse uses eye and head movements to actively explore its environment is largely unknown. To answer this outstanding question, we used a system that we recently developed (Meyer et al., Neuron 2018) to track head and eye movements in freely moving mice. We found that the complex head and eye movement patterns in mice can be decomposed into two distinct, independent components each linking eye and head but in different ways. These two types eye-head coupling were maintained during visually-guided behaviors. Additionally, this work resolved an apparent discrepancy between eye movements observed in head-restrained and freely moving mice, demonstrating that eye movements in both conditions are invariably associated with either planned or actual head movement (Meyer, et al., bioRxiv 2020).
