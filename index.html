I am a Radboud Excellence Fellow at the Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition, and Behaviour (Nijmegen, the Netherlands) and a research fellow at the Sainsbury Wellcome Centre for Neural Circuits and Behaviour (London, UK).
My goal is to understand how humans and other animals gather sensory information while interacting with their environment. For example, without apparent effort, our brains guide us our way to work even though thousands of cyclists (e.g., in Nijmegen) or pedestrians (e.g., in London) that need to be detected (and avoided) within the fraction of a second. To do so, our brains combine sensory information coming from our environment with a wide range of signals, including vestibular and motor signals, coming from our own movements. My goal is to understand how these signals are combined with sensory information in the brain to allow flexible and robust behaviors.
To reveal the underlying neural computations I combine experiments in mice with computational modeling (borrowing techniques from machine learning). To measure to the full range of sensory and movement-related signals, I perform experiments in animals that are free to move. As a first step, I developed a system enabling head and eye tracking with concomitant neural recordings in freely moving mice (Meyer et al., Neuron 2018). I recently used this system to reveal the precise head and eye movement (gaze) patterns in freely moving mice (Meyer et al., bioRxiv). To investigate the range and function of movement-related signals in the early visual system, I am currently combining the developed system with virtual reality in freely moving animals.
